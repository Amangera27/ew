Introduction

Electrical safety is one of the most overlooked yet most critical aspects of owning or managing a property. Whether it’s a home, a business, or an industrial facility, the electrical installation behind the walls determines the safety of everyone who steps inside. A single fault—an overloaded circuit, poor earthing, deteriorated insulation—can trigger dangerous electrical shocks, equipment failures, or devastating fires. These incidents are far more common than people realise, and many could have been prevented with one simple step: ensuring the installation has a valid electrical Certificate of Compliance (COC).

A COC is a legally required document confirming that the electrical installation has been inspected and tested and meets the national electrical safety standard. It is more than paperwork; it is a safeguard that directly protects lives, prevents property damage, and reduces financial risk. This blog explores how a valid COC works, what it certifies, why legality and qualifications matter, the real dangers it prevents, how to verify authenticity, and the practical steps for obtaining one. Each section provides clear, actionable guidance to help homeowners, landlords, and businesses understand their obligations and responsibilities, and explains why choosing trained professionals—such as those trained through TDMI—makes a meaningful difference to long-term electrical safety.

What a COC Actually Certifies (What Inspectors Test)

The Scope of an Inspection: From Point of Control to Point of Consumption

A COC is not just a signature or a quick glance at your electrical board. It is the final confirmation of a thorough, structured inspection and testing process. The inspection covers the entire electrical installation, starting from the point where electrical supply enters a property, through the distribution board, and down to the individual circuits, sockets, lights, and fixed appliances. Inspectors must ensure that every part of the installation complies with the national wiring standard. This holistic approach reduces the chances of hidden faults remaining undetected.

During the inspection, registered professionals assess the condition of wiring, check for deterioration, and confirm that all connections are secure. They also ensure that cables are sized correctly to carry their designated loads. Proper cable sizing prevents overheating and reduces the risk of insulation breakdown, which is a common cause of electrical fires. Inspectors will also examine whether circuits have been incorrectly added by previous owners, whether any DIY connections are present, and whether the property has undergone modifications that require updated compliance.

Typical Items on the Test Report

The COC is accompanied by a detailed test report. This report contains specific test results, confirming that each circuit and protective device meets the required safety values. Inspectors typically verify:

  • Earthing and bonding continuity, ensuring exposed metal parts cannot become live
  • Correct operation of residual current devices (RCDs), which protect against electric shock
  • Overcurrent and short-circuit protection at the distribution board
  • Polarity of sockets and switches
  • Insulation resistance to detect deteriorated wiring
  • Condition of surge protection or other safety components

This meticulous testing process is the backbone of the COC. Each test reduces a specific hazard: RCDs protect against fatal shock, correct earthing prevents dangerous voltages on metalwork, and proper insulation resistance ensures circuits will not arc or heat dangerously. By signing the COC, the registered professional legally confirms that every test has been completed and that the installation meets the required standard on the date of issue.

The Regulatory Framework: SANS, OHS Regulations and Legal Requirements

The Legal Authority Behind the COC

South Africa’s electrical safety system is governed through the national Occupational Health and Safety framework. Within it, the Electrical Installation Regulations set out strict rules requiring that electrical installations must be inspected, tested, and certified by a registered person. This legal requirement ensures that the people issuing COCs are qualified, accountable, and competent in applying the national electrical standard. No property may legally be sold or significantly altered without a valid COC confirming the safety of its installation.

SANS 10142-1 as the Technical Benchmark

The core technical standard for electrical installations is SANS 10142-1. It sets out every requirement—from cable selection and earth fault protection to switchboard layout and safety clearances. A COC confirms that the installation complies with this standard. SANS 10142-1 is updated periodically, reflecting new technology and new knowledge around electrical hazards. For this reason, homeowners and property managers should understand that a COC is valid for the date of issue but may require re-inspection after major work, damage, or if the installation has deteriorated over time. Staying aligned with updated standards ensures continued safety and reduces long-term liability.

Who Can Issue a COC (Registered Persons) and Why Credentials Matter

Registered Electricians and Required Authorisations

Only qualified, registered electricians may legally issue a COC. This requirement protects consumers from receiving fraudulent, incomplete, or unsafe compliance documents. Registered persons must hold the appropriate trade qualifications, a wireman’s licence, and registration with the relevant authorities. They must also demonstrate competence in applying the national wiring code and interpreting the standard during inspections.

Unregistered individuals may perform minor repairs under certain conditions, but they cannot certify an installation. If they attempt to issue a COC, the document is invalid and leaves the property owner legally exposed. Property owners can protect themselves by requesting to see the electrician’s registration documents, ID and registration number, and confirmation of the authorised scope.

How to Verify a Contractor

Before hiring an inspector, property owners should ask direct, clear questions. For example:

“Can you show your wireman’s licence and registration number?”
“Will you provide a full test report together with the COC?”
“Does your registration allow you to work on this type of installation?”

Authentic registered persons never hesitate to provide this information. It is also advisable to note the electrician’s name, company, registration number, and COC serial number for record-keeping. Using a registered professional ensures the installation is thoroughly assessed and that the COC will stand up to legal and insurance scrutiny.

How a Valid COC Directly Protects Lives

Shock Prevention Through Proper Testing

One of the greatest risks in any electrical installation is electric shock. Even low levels of fault current can cause severe injury or be fatal. A valid COC confirms that residual current devices (RCDs), earthing systems, bonding conductors, and protective devices are functioning correctly. These components work together to ensure that if a fault occurs—such as a live conductor touching a metal appliance—the protective devices disconnect the power immediately to prevent bodily harm.

Inspectors test the speed and sensitivity of RCDs, check the resistance of the earthing system, and confirm that bonding is continuous across all metalwork. This ensures that human contact with faulty equipment is unlikely to result in electric shock. A single failed device or deteriorated earth connection could mean the difference between a harmless trip and a fatal incident.

Reducing Severe and Fatal Injuries

Electrical shock incidents often happen silently. A child may touch a faulty appliance, or a worker may come into contact with exposed wiring. Without proper protection, the consequences can be catastrophic. By ensuring all circuits meet the required disconnection times and safety thresholds, a valid COC reduces the likelihood of severe injuries. Investing in proper compliance creates a safer environment for families, tenants, employees, and contractors who may work on the installation in future.

How a Valid COC Protects Property and Reduces Fire Risk

Overcurrent Protection and Thermal Hazards

Most electrical fires begin with overheating—caused by overloaded circuits, incorrect cable sizing, loose connections, or deteriorated insulation. When circuits are not properly protected, small faults can escalate into smouldering fires behind walls or in ceilings. The inspection process behind the COC identifies these risks before they cause damage.

For example, a property may have been extended without adding new distribution circuits. This creates overloaded wiring that heats up during use. A trained inspector will detect this during testing, and the resulting COC process forces corrective action, preventing future fires.

The Financial Impact of Non-Compliance

Fires linked to electrical faults can destroy homes, businesses, and irreplaceable belongings. Insurance companies frequently request a valid COC when processing claims involving electrical faults. Without one, property owners may face delays or disputes. A valid COC serves as proof that the installation was safe and legally compliant at the time of inspection, helping protect property owners financially in the event of an incident.

Typical Scenarios Where a COC Prevented Harm

Residential Property Transfers and Inspections

In many home sales, the COC inspection becomes the moment when hidden dangers come to light. Consider a property where a previous owner made DIY alterations—adding extra plugs, extending circuits, or moving lights. When the registered inspector conducts the COC assessment, they often uncover faults such as reversed polarity, missing earthing, or illegal jointing in ceilings. In one real-world example, an inspector identified an illegally added sub-main connection with no protective device. If left undetected, it could have caused a serious fire during high-load use. The COC process prevented a potential disaster.

Commercial and Industrial Facilities

In a small workshop, an inspector discovered overloaded motor circuits with incorrect breakers and deteriorated insulation caused by continuous operation. Without correction, a phase-to-earth fault could have occurred at any time, threatening employees and equipment. After testing, the faulty circuits were repaired, the correct protective devices were installed, and a valid COC was issued. This example illustrates how COC inspections serve not only as legal obligations but also as essential risk-management tools for business owners.

Rental Properties and Tenant Protection

Rental properties often see repeated use of appliances, temporary installations, and occasional DIY interference. A routine COC inspection in a rental home uncovered a damaged cable hidden behind a kitchen unit. Tenants had reported occasional tripping, but the underlying cause was a serious insulation breakdown. After repair, the property once again met the required safety standard—highlighting how regular compliance checks protect tenant safety and reduce landlord liability.

Common Myths and Pitfalls: Fake, Expired or Misused COCs

Fake or Fraudulent COCs

Unfortunately, fraudulent COCs do exist. These documents often appear legitimate but are issued by unqualified individuals who bypass proper testing. Property owners may unknowingly accept these certificates during sales or renovations, believing their installation is safe. A fake COC provides zero actual protection and exposes the owner to legal and financial consequences.

Signs of a questionable COC include missing test reports, no unique serial number, poor-quality printed documents, or reluctance by the “issuer” to share registration details. Any suspicion should be addressed immediately by requesting verification or commissioning a new inspection from a registered professional.

Expired or Partial COCs

Another common misconception is that a COC is valid forever. In reality, the certificate reflects compliance only at the time of testing. If the installation undergoes renovations, damage, or modifications, the certificate may no longer reflect the true condition. Partial COCs also cause confusion; these certificates apply only to specific sections of the installation, such as a newly added circuit. Property owners must confirm whether the certificate covers the entire installation or only a portion.

Understanding the difference protects owners from assuming their property is fully compliant when it may not be.

How Often Should You Re-Check or Re-Test?

Validity and Triggers for Re-Testing

There is no universal expiry date for all COCs, but several circumstances require re-inspection:

  • Renovations, extensions, or major electrical work
  • Evidence of damage such as water ingress or fire
  • Changes to load requirements or equipment
  • Property transfers where regulations require a new assessment

Even without these triggers, regular inspections are recommended to ensure ongoing safety, especially in older installations.

Best Practice Schedule

Homeowners can consider re-inspecting every few years to ensure continued compliance. Landlords benefit from re-testing between tenants, as installations can deteriorate quickly under heavy use. Businesses should assess their installations according to their risk profile, considering operational hours, equipment type, and environmental conditions. Regular inspections reduce risk and maintain electrical safety long term.

Practical Steps: How to Get a COC and What to Expect

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining a COC follows a clear, structured process:

  • Contact a registered electrician experienced with compliance inspections.
  • Schedule a full inspection of the installation.
  • Allow the inspector to conduct tests on circuits, protective devices, and wiring integrity.
  • Review any faults identified in the test report and approve the recommended repairs.
  • Once all remedial work is complete, the inspector issues the COC together with the signed test report.

This process ensures transparency and allows property owners to understand the condition of their installation. Keeping both the COC and test report safely stored is essential for future property transfers or insurance claims.

Understanding Cost Drivers

Although cost varies across regions and installations, what matters most is quality and compliance. Repairs identified during the inspection form the bulk of costs, especially if outdated wiring or overloaded circuits are discovered. Property owners should request itemised quotes, ask questions about recommended upgrades, and remember that investment in safety always outweighs the cost of repairing fire or shock damage.
